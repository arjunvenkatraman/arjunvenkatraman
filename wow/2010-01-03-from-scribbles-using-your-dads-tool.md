Isaac Asimov once wrote a story about a future where humans, so utterly dependent on computers, had lost the basic ability to calculate. The people in that story didn't know they had lost something — they simply could not conceive of their world without the scaffolding that had always been there.

If Michael Crichton were a dinosaur writing *Jurassic Park* during the Jurassic period, contemporary dinosaurs would find predictions of their extinction laughable. It is inconceivable to people that what they hold to be basic axioms of existence will change.

My generation left home understanding we faced a harsh world without safety nets. We had to master "life ke funde" — life's basic truths:

- Don't get into situations you can't get out of
- Cash is always a problem
- Nobody is coming to save you
- If you want something done, learn to do it yourself

Modern children inhabit cushioned environments created by people who internalized these harsh lessons. They're not lesser — they have different foundational knowledge. The progression: from "Where's my next meal?" to "Is 40 GB enough storage?"

But here's what concerns me. My father theorized that the most successful people emerge from impoverished backgrounds because "people who come from poor/uneducated/violent backgrounds have nowhere else to go but up." Success requires dissatisfaction with current circumstances. Privilege creates complacency.

What would I teach my own children? Mathematics, English, and the art of being dissatisfied — deliberately cultivating the discontent that motivates upward mobility.
